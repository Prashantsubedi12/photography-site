Why I Carry My Camera Everywhere

The best shots don't announce themselves. They just happen — and you either have your camera or you don't. Here's the mindset shift that changed everything.

It was a Wednesday afternoon last October. I was heading to the konbini near Nakanoshima and I saw this older man feeding pigeons under the bridge — the light was cutting through the arch perfectly, long shadows, everything. No camera. Just my phone, which was almost dead.

I got one blurry frame before the phone shut off. That was the moment I made a rule for myself: the camera goes everywhere I go. No exceptions.

The Problem with 'I'll Bring It Next Time'

Most photographers carry their camera to photo shoots. That's a completely different thing from carrying your camera through life.

When you only bring your camera when you 'intend to shoot', you're editing the world before you've even looked at it. You're deciding in advance what's worth photographing. And you'll always be wrong, because the best moments are the ones you weren't planning for.

The best photo you'll ever take is probably happening right now, somewhere you didn't think to look.

What Changed When I Started Carrying It Daily

When the camera becomes a constant, something shifts in how you see. You start noticing light before you notice anything else. The angle of sun on a building. The reflection in a puddle after rain. The way two people stand near each other in a train station.

None of this requires a shoot. It just requires being present — and having your camera.

I also got faster. Not technically faster — I'm not some sports photographer tracking burst shots. I mean I got faster at recognizing a frame and lifting the camera before the moment disappears. That only comes from practice. Daily practice. Not once-a-week practice.

The Weight Problem (and the Real Answer)

I know the objection. The camera is heavy. The bag is annoying. You're going to a casual dinner, not a photo walk.

I've thought about this a lot. My answer is: the weight is the point. Carrying something heavy every day that you chose to carry is different from carrying something heavy that was imposed on you. It's a daily commitment. A reminder of what matters to you.

Photography pulled me out of a very dark place. It gave me a reason to leave the apartment when I didn't have one. When I'm walking around Osaka with my camera, I'm not just a person going somewhere — I'm a photographer looking for something. That shift in identity matters more than any specific photo I might take.

I turned the heaviest thing in my life into the most beautiful. If I can do that, so can you.

Practical Advice (If You Want It)

Here's what actually works for me:

  • Keep the camera in the smallest bag that fits it. Friction is the enemy of consistency.
  • Set a single rule: camera comes on any trip that takes more than 20 minutes. Non-negotiable.
  • Shoot bad photos. Shoot boring photos. Shoot when the light is terrible. The habit is more important than the result, especially at the start.
  • Review your photos every few days. Not to edit them — just to look. You'll start seeing what you're drawn to. That's your style forming.

The camera you have is the camera that takes photos. The camera at home doesn't take any.

Portraits April 28, 2026

What I'm Really Doing When I Photograph Someone

A good portrait isn't about the light or the lens. It's about making someone feel safe enough to stop performing and just be. That's the job.

The first thing most people do when a camera points at them is perform. Not their real face — a camera face. The camera smile. The slightly straighter back. The held breath. You can feel it happen in real time.

The photo I want is the one that comes after all of that drops away. My job, from the moment I meet someone until that moment, is to make that happen as quickly as possible.

Why I Talk So Much During a Shoot

People think portrait photography is mostly about the camera. They see the camera. They assume it's the main tool. It's not. The main tool is conversation.

I talk constantly during sessions — about where they're from, what they're doing in Osaka, what they thought of the food. Not because I need the information. Because when someone is genuinely answering a question they're interested in, their face does something different. The calculation leaves. Something more real takes its place.

The shutter goes during that.

A portrait is not a photo of how someone looks. It's a photo of how they feel when they forget you're looking.

Why Anxiety Made Me Better at This

I have social anxiety disorder. Clinically diagnosed, medicated, the whole thing. There was a period where leaving my apartment felt genuinely impossible.

Because of that, I know exactly what it feels like to be watched. To feel exposed. To be in a situation where you believe everyone is judging you.

When someone stands in front of my camera, they are in that situation. Most photographers are thinking about light. I'm thinking about that too — but I'm also thinking about what I can do to make this person feel like they're not being judged. Like they're just spending time with someone who thinks they're interesting.

That empathy — which came from the hardest years of my life — is the most useful thing I bring to a portrait session.

The Moment I'm Waiting For

Every session has a turning point. It's usually about 20–30 minutes in. The subject stops thinking about what their face is doing. They start just being where they are. Their shoulders drop a centimetre. Something in the eyes changes.

That's when I take the photos I actually care about. Everything before that is warmup — necessary warmup, but warmup.

The technical part — exposure, focus, composition — I've done so many times it's automatic. What I'm actually doing during a session is managing the emotional state of the room. Creating the conditions where real photos can happen.

What This Means for You

If you've ever had a photo taken of you and thought 'that doesn't look like me' — it's not your face. It's that you were performing, and the camera caught the performance.

My job is to get to the photo before that. The photo of you that looks like you — not like you trying to look like you.

That's worth doing. I think everyone deserves at least one photograph that feels true.
